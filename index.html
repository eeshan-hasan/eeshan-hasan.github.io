eehasan@iu.edu

I am a dual PhD Cognitive Science and Psychology student, advised by Jennifer Trueblood at Indiana University. Previously, I finished my Masters in Psychology at Vanderbilt University and Masters in Mathematics from the University of Hyderabad, India. I worked for a year in a healthcare analytics startup as a Mathematician/Machine Learning Specialist.

I adopt a joint computational and experimental approach to better understand cognitive decision making processes. I have studied a wide range of decisions ranging from multi-attribute choice, consumer decisions, medical decisions and social media decisions. In my research I have used statistics, approximate Bayesian inference, evidence accumulation models and machine learning (deep learning) models. Some of my broad questions are.

  1. Training novices for specialized tasks and the wisdom of the novice crowd? I find that humans are adaptive and learn to do specialized tasks such as medical decision making and detecting cognitive distortions with little training. In fact, when put together groups of novices can outperform experts in some medical tasks.
  2. What is the relationship between machine (deep learning) intelligence and human intelligence? There are intriguing similarities and differences between human and machine intelligence. We can use this relationship to understand, improve and augment human decisions. Intriguingly, in recent work we find that novices can represent features of complex-real world stimuli in ways similar to domain-specific neural networks (in prep). Also, one can resolve inconsistencies in decisions made by a human by looking at decisions on similar images as determined by neural network representations.
  3. How does attention impact decisions? It is impossible for us to consider all of the information around us. Thus, we rely on attention to focus and intelligently select information. I am interested in characterizing the nature of the attentional processes and studying its impact on behavior. We show how manipulating attention by presentation formats and bottom up processes such as salience impacts decisions.

Projects

Deep Learning Representations and Human Representations

Using deep learning machine representations to understand and improve human decisions.

Artificial neural networks can give us representations of complex real-world images, expanding the scope of cognitive computational modeling beyond boring lab stimuli that have simple representations. My research uses these representations to better understand and augment human decision-making. We demonstrate how medical-image classification decisions can be de-noised by aggregating decisions on similar images. In our algorithm, the final decision on a target image is cancerous if a percentage t of the k most similar images are cancerous, else it is not cancerous. Similarity between images is calculated as the distance between representations from an artificial neural network. We show that this algorithm works for novices but not experts, indicating difference in decision making processes for the two populations (Hasan et al. 2022a , 2022b ). More recently, I find that novice representations are more like domain-specific CNNs trained on cancer cell classification showing that human representations are flexible and can meaningfully extract information from domain-specific stimuli.

The Role of Visual Attention in Decision Making

Multi-alternative Multi-attribute Choice

I am curious about how bounded resources such as visual attention might impact choice. To study the impact of presentation format on choice, we designed a registered report with a large-scale experiment with over 2000 individuals 24 different condition, comprehensively manipulating the presentation format. We find that the order robustly impacts choice for the attraction effect by potentially changing the comparisons that are made (Hasan et al. 2024) . Using a mechanistic process model to compare mechanisms, we find that bottom-up attention by making options salient impact choice in different ways by creating a selection boost (Hasan et al. 2024) . This highlights the complexity in the role of attention in choice.

Wisdom of the Crowds

How do we harness collective intelligence for complex (e.g., medical) tasks?

According to the wisdom of the crowd phenomenon, the aggregated decision of a group is more accurate than the decision of an average decision-maker. In medical decision making, we find the wisdom of the inner crowd effect, where repeated decisions improves individual classification accuracy for both novices and experts. Building on these results, we show that aggregation algorithms based on confidence weighting scale to larger groups of participants, dramatically improving diagnostic accuracy, with the performance of groups of novices reaching that of individual experts Hasan et al. (2023). We collaborate with Centaur Labs, which recruits individuals with varying expertise to label medical datasets on an app-based interface. There was a large dispersion in the geographical location, experience, training, and performance of the recruited individuals. We tested several wisdom of the crowd algorithms of varying complexity from a simple unweighted average to more complex Bayesian models that account for individual patterns of errors. Using a switchboard analysis, we observe that the best-performing algorithms rely on selecting top performers, weighting decisions by training accuracy, and take into account the task environment. These algorithms far exceed expert performance. Hasan et al. (2024)

Social Media Interactions

What is social media doing to us? Can we do better?

Social Media has changed the way we interact with each other. It has created an environment with complex interactions between human, algorithms and other entities. How does this affect cognition and behavior? In one paper, we focus on cognitive distortions or distorted thinking is the use of exaggerated unrealistic ways of thinking and speaking which is associated with mental illnesses such as depression and schizophrenia. Social media, parallelly, has a heightened use of language associated with cognitive distortions. My research examines this association between mental illness and social media use in a controlled experimental environment. We investigated the impact of teaching individuals about cognitive distortions and studied the change in decisions to interact with online social media content. Hasan et al. (Under Review) .

Computational Methods

I love learning about new computational techniques and associated debates. These are my current favorite approaches.

Generalized Linear Mixed Models

Fixed and Random Effects.

Environments and experiments are often structured. They contain several decisions from the same person and several decisions on the same item. In my work, I found it extremely powerful to exploit this structure by treating participant and experimental items as random effects. I especially like the libraries in R for this sort of analysis.

Artificial Neural Networks

Machine Representations/ Embeddings.

The internal layers of a neural network contain important information about the stimulus. I am interested in exploiting this geometric structure and explore the correspondence between humans and machines. Recently, I built myself a deep learning PC to explore this further. I am comfortable especially with PyTorch and have trained convolutional neural networks, transformers, GANs etc,.

Evidence Accumulation Models

Decision after a threshold.

In evidence accumulation models, evidence is continuously accumulated until a threshold of evidence is accumulated after which a decision is triggered. Within this framework, one might make different models to explain what the nature of this evidence is, how it is accumulated and when a decision is triggered. This has allowed us to explore all sorts of decision biases and the role of attention in decision making. I typically use python and custom code for this sort of analysis.

Bayesian MCMC Methods

Quantifying Uncertainty.

Bayesian models give us the uncertainty in our estimates. I have used PyMC3 for most of my Bayesian Analysis. I have also coded up my own differential evolution MC sampler. I loved looking under the hood to see how these sampling algorithms work and the kind of issues they may have. My experience also made me realize concerns about issues such as inference with correlated parameters, bimodal distributions etc. which are issues with inference in general (not just Bayesian inference).
